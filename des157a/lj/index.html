Game Design UX Best Practices Review

Reading this article raises conflicting feelings in me. As an avid gamer, many of the pro-tips the author provides regarding advertisements and stores out are things that I hate the most about games. But, as a game developer, I see the logic behind his advice. Most of the article focuses on mobile game development, but there is a wealth of information that can be generalized as well. The author touches on points such as user screen reachability, popups, advertisements, videos, draggable objects, rewarded videos, and more.

Through my experience of this class, I have come to realize that UI/UX enhancements are very subtle, things that normally developers wouldn't think about, but ultimately add up to create an amazing user experience. The tips given in this article aren't any different. One of my most favorite (and hated) subtle tips is regarding the advertisement popups and store open buttons, where the author states that these should be activated on user touch, whereas normally interactive items on a phone screen are activated on user touch-leave. Another interesting tip was to remove the cancel button on popups since users associate that with 'annoying' content, which is counter-intuitive to the modal overlay article advice which says to always give users an escape hatch. However, the advice does make sense.

These tips really do help increase our critical thinking skills regarding taking into account the little things, and I look forward to implementing them in our game (definitely not the advertisements though)!

Best Practices for Dialog Windows Review

This is an interesting article which talks about the best practices for using overlays in our design. It made sense that back when popups were first introduced, they were extremely useful. Nowadays though, popups are the bane of every user's existence, and the author correctly informs us that the audience will reflexively try to close the window. The article lets us know how to avoid raising the user's ire, while also making the experience as accessible and better for them as possible.

The article talks about when are the appropriate moments to use modal windows, like when we need to show bigger images or videos. It teaches us about the best practices for implementing a modal page, like giving the users an easy escape, adding simple buttons, properly sizing the window, and adding focus. The article also includes tips and tricks for implementing mobile overlays, which is a huge help!

Extending on inclusion, the blog post also gives us great ideas to help make the window more accessible for everyone, like making the popup keyboard accessible, using ARIA tags, and keeping low-vision users in mind as well. This article was super informative and I really look forward to using these tips when I make an overlay!

16th Oct, 2021

Best Practices for Form Design Review

The article talks about best form practices, which in my mind, originally meant something related to design and presentation. While that does present as something to keep in mind, a surprising focus is on human psychology. In hindsight, it does make a lot of sense. A form is more than something that should look appealing to the user; it is an experience. I can see a lot of these points making form filling an infinitely better experience for the user. I should know since I have been filling out tons of job application forms online. The next time I have a chance to add suggestions to my experience, I'll take the opportunity to link this article.

The article teaches us about numerous improvements, which might seem small but elevate the user experience. I especially agree with splitting the form into multiple chunks for it to be more appealing. Adding labels to each portion and a progress bar was something I wished a lot of my job applications would have. The article also opened my eyes to create a form that was inclusive for everyone, like adding an exclamation sign next to an alert message to allow color-blind people to understand the context of an alert. The best part of the article is that these changes are simple to implement but infinitely improve the end-user experience while keeping it inclusive of all people. I'll definitely keep these in mind while designing the mad-libs game next week!

As for my best form-filling experience in recent memory, it goes to the job application forms from jobs.lever.co. The forms here were simple, clean, and follow many principles that the article proposes. They kept checkboxes instead of dropdown forms whenever there were fewer options, kept the complicated questions for the end, and were Tab-Ready.
